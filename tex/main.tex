\section{Introduction}
Image acquisition from optical systems are, in most cases, blurred due to the imperfection of the optical system.
Estimating the blur is essential for manual or self calibration of optical systems, especially for microscopic and astronomical imaging.
This blur have been theoretically and experimentally proven to be varying in space, due to several reasons, such as the design of the considered optical system, the movement of the sample and the refactive index variation of the sample.
This blur is commonly called space varying impulse response (SVIR), some methods to estimate SVIRs are to the image into small patches and the blur is assumed to be invariant within each patch, a optimization method is then used to estimate the blur for each patch, often with the help of some regularization terms, such as the spatial smoothness of the blur across different patches. Other methods approximate the SVIR by its projection onto a low-dimensional subspace, which is spanned by a set of basis functions and then estimate the coefficients of the projection \cite{9311840}. 

To abord the problem with caution, we begin by introducing mathematical formulation in section 2. We present the invariant blur estimation, which is a simple case of SVIR estimation in section 3, and then we move to the SVIR estimation in section 4. 

\section{Problem Formulation}
\subsection{Modelization}

Let $U_i$ and $U_o$ are the complex field of the image and sample respectively, \cite{goodman1969introduction} states that their relation can be expressed via a integral operator $H$:
\begin{equation}
    U_i(x) = (HU_o)(x) = \int_{\mathbb{R}^2} G(x,x') U_o(x') dx'
\end{equation}
$G$ is called the integral kernel of $H$.

In most optical systems, the measurement is the intensity of the image complex field:
\begin{equation}
    y(x) = |U_i(x)|^2 + \boldsymbol{e}(x) =  \left|\int_{\mathbb{R}^2} G(x,x') U_o(x') dx'\right|^2 + \boldsymbol{e}(x)
\end{equation}
where $\boldsymbol{e}(x)$ is a gaussian noise, i.e $\boldsymbol{e}(x) \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2)$, and $\boldsymbol{e}(x)$ and $\boldsymbol{e}(x')$ are i.i.d for all $x \neq z $.

It is well-known that the case of incoherent light, the intensity of the sum of complex fields is equal to the sum of the intensity of the individual fields, 
\begin{equation}
    y(x) = \int_{\mathbb{R}^2} |G(x,x')|^2 \, |U_o(x')|^2 dx' + \boldsymbol{e}(x)
\end{equation}
The function $S(., x') = G(\cdot + x', x')$ is called the space varying impulse response (SVIR) of the integral operator $H$. The light intensity $y$ can then be written as:
\begin{equation}
    y(x) = \int_{\mathbb{R}^2} |S(x-x',x')|^2 |U_o(x')|^2 dx' + \boldsymbol{e}(x)
\end{equation}
 
We assume that the SVIR $S$ satisfies the smooth variation property, i.e $S$ is a smooth function with respect to the second variable.

The objective of introducing the SVIR $S$ is to approximate the original integral operator by a convolution operator by taking into account the slow spatical variation of the impulse response.
Throught experiments, one should find that the SVIR $S$ is a smooth function of the second variable and a compactly supported function of the first variable. Therefore, one can approximate the SVIR $S$ by a convolution operator with a compactly supported kernel,
\begin{equation}
    S(x-x',x') \approx h(x-x', x) \quad \mbox{ for } x' \mbox{ in neighborhood of } x
\end{equation} 
where $h:\mathbb{R}^2 \times \mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}$ is suppported in ball of radius $f_c$ and center at origin with respect to the first variable, i.e a supp($h(\cdot, x)$) = $\mathcal{B}_{f_c},\, \forall x\in \mathbb{R}^2$. 
This approximation yeilds:
\begin{align}
    y(x) &= \int_{\mathbb{R}^2} |h(x-x', x)|^2 |U_o(x')|^2 dx' + \boldsymbol{e}(x)\\
    &= \left(|h(\cdot, x)|^2 \ast |U_o|^2\right)(x) + \boldsymbol{e}(x) 
\end{align}

The kernel $h(\cdot, x)$ is called the point spread function (PSF), while its modulus square $|h(\cdot, x)|^2$ is called the amplitude spread function (ASF).
In the following, we consider two approaches for modeling the PSF $h$ and ASF $|h|^2$: physics-informed modelization and physics-agnostic modelization.

\subsubsection{PSF as the fourier transform of pupil function (physics-informed modelization)}
In the context of microscopic imaging, the PSF is, up to a constant factor, to fourier transform of the pupil function of the optical system, which is modeled as:
\begin{equation}
    P(\xi, x) = \rho(\xi, x) e^{-i 2\pi \phi(\xi, x)} \quad \mbox{ for } \xi \in \mathbb{R}^2 \mbox{ and } x \in \mathbb{R}^2
\end{equation} 
where $\rho(\cdot, x)$ is the aperture function of the optical system, and $\phi(\cdot, x)$ is the phase aberration of the optical system. Intuitively, $\rho(\cdot, x)$ can be seen as an amplitude mask, i,e $\rho(\xi, x) \leq 1$ for $\xi$ inside the aperture and $\rho(\xi, x) = 0$ for $\xi$ outside the aperture, while $\phi(\cdot, x)$ can be seen as a phase modification of the light wavefront caused by the optical system, including the lens, mirrors, etc,... 

The phase aberration $\phi(\cdot, x)$ can be approximated by a linear combination of Zernike polynomials, which are a complete orthogonal basis for the unit disk:
\begin{equation}
    \phi(\xi, x)  \approx \phi^{K, \boldsymbol{\alpha}}(\xi, x) = \sum_{k=1}^{K} \alpha_k(x) Z_k^{f_D}(\xi) 
\end{equation}
where $Z_k^{f_D}$ is the $k$-th Zernike polynomial defined on the disk of radius $f_D$, and $\alpha_k$ is the coefficient of the linear combination.

For the sake of simplicity, we assume that the aperture function $\rho(\cdot, x)$ is an indicator function of a disk with radius $f_D$, i.e 
\begin{equation}
    \rho(\xi, .) = \begin{cases}
        1 & \mbox{ if } \xi \in \mathcal{B}_{f_D} \\
        0 & \mbox{ otherwise }
    \end{cases} 
\end{equation}

The PSF $h(\cdot, x)$ can be then approximated by taking the Fourier transform of the pupil function $P(\cdot, x)^{K, \boldsymbol{\alpha}}$:
\begin{align}
    h(z, x) \approx h^{K, \boldsymbol{\alpha}}(z, x) &= \int_{\mathbb{R}^2} P^{K, \boldsymbol{\alpha}}(\xi, x) \exp \left(i 2\pi \xi \cdot z\right) d\xi \\
    & = \int_{\mathcal{B}_{f_D}} \exp (- i 2\pi \phi^{K, \boldsymbol{\alpha}}(\xi, x)) \, \exp \left(-i 2\pi \, \xi \cdot z\right) d\xi
\end{align}
Also, the conjugate of the PSF $h^{K, \boldsymbol{\alpha}}$ is given by:
\begin{align}
    \overline{h^{K, \boldsymbol{\alpha}}(z, x)} &= \int_{\mathbb{R}^2} \overline{P^{K, \boldsymbol{\alpha}}(\xi, x) \exp \left(-i 2\pi \xi \cdot z\right)} d\xi \\
    &= \int_{\mathcal{B}_{f_D}} \exp (i 2 \pi \phi^{K, \boldsymbol{\alpha}}(\xi, x)) \, \exp \left(i 2\pi \, \xi \cdot z\right) d\xi \\
    &= \int_{\mathcal{B}_{f_D}} \exp (i 2 \pi \phi^{K, \boldsymbol{\alpha}}(- \xi, x)) \, \exp \left(-i 2\pi \, (\xi) \cdot z\right) d\xi \\
    &=: \hat{Q}^{K, \boldsymbol{\alpha}}(z, x)
\end{align}
where $Q^{K, \boldsymbol{\alpha}}$ is defined as:
\begin{equation}
    Q^{K, \boldsymbol{\alpha}}(\xi,x) = \begin{cases}
        \exp (i 2 \pi \phi^{K, \boldsymbol{\alpha}}(-\xi, x)) & \mbox{ if } \xi \in \mathcal{B}_{f_D} \\
        0 & \mbox{ otherwise }
    \end{cases}
\end{equation}
The ASF $|h^{K, \boldsymbol{\alpha}}(z, x)|^2$ is approximated by:
\begin{align}
    |h^{K, \boldsymbol{\alpha}}(z, x)|^2 &= h^{K, \boldsymbol{\alpha}}(z, x) \overline{h^{K, \boldsymbol{\alpha}}(z, x)} \\
    &= \hat{P}^{K, \boldsymbol{\alpha}}(z, x) \hat{Q}^{K, \boldsymbol{\alpha}}(z, x) \\
\end{align}




To simplify the notation, we denote $\omega = |h^{K, \boldsymbol{\alpha}}|^2$ as the ASF at location $x$. The measurement $y$ can be then approximated by:
\begin{equation}
  y(x) \approx \left(\omega(\cdot, x) \ast |U_o|^2\right)(x) + \boldsymbol{e}(x)
\end{equation}

\textbf{Note}: In this report, when doing the fourier transform, we only do it with respect to the first variable.

\subsubsection{Pixel-wise modelization of ASF (physics-agnostic modelization)}
In this approach, we directly model the ASF $\omega(.,x)$ at each spatial location $x$ as the softmax of values of a 2d grid of size $P \times P$, i.e
\begin{equation}
    \omega(\boldsymbol{z}_k, x) = softmax(\boldsymbol{z}_{k=1}^{P^2})[k] 
\end{equation}
This approach ensure, by construction, the non-negativity and normalization of the ASF at each spatial location $x$. 


\subsection{Optimization problem}
Our objective is to estimate the coefficients $\boldsymbol{\alpha}$ in order to recover the PSF $h^{K, \boldsymbol{\alpha}}$ and ASF $|h^{K, \boldsymbol{\alpha}}|^2$. To achieve this, we aim to minimize the following objective function:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\alpha}; \lambda) = F(\boldsymbol{\alpha}) + \lambda R(\boldsymbol{\alpha})
\end{equation}
where $F(\boldsymbol{\alpha})$ is the data fidelity term, $R(\boldsymbol{\alpha})$ is the regularization term, and $\lambda$ is a term that controls the trade-off between the data fidelity term and the regularization term.

The data fidelity term $F(\boldsymbol{\alpha})$ can be expressed as:
\begin{equation}
    F(\boldsymbol{\alpha}) = \int_{\mathbb{R}^2} \left|y(x) - \left(\omega(\cdot, x) \ast |U_o|^2\right)(x)\right|^2 dx
\end{equation}


The regularization term $R(\boldsymbol{\alpha})$ is:
\begin{equation}
    R(\boldsymbol{\alpha}) = \begin{cases}
        +\infty &\mbox{ if } \exists x \in \mathbb{R}^2 \mbox{s.t} \int_{\mathbb{R}^2} \omega(z,x) dz \neq 1 \mbox{, or } \exists (z,x) \in \mathbb{R}^2 \times \mathbb{R}^2 \mbox{s.t} \omega(z,x) < 0 \\
        \int_{\mathbb{R}^2} \|\nabla_x \omega^{K, \boldsymbol{\alpha}}(\cdot, x)\|_2^2 dx & \mbox{ otherwise }
    \end{cases}
\end{equation}
This regularization term ensures the non-negativity and normalization of the ASF at each spatial location $x$, and also promotes the smooth variation of the ASF across different spatial locations.

\section{Spatially invariant impulse response estimation}
This section focuses on the estimation of the spatially invariant impulse response, i.e, the case where the SVIR $S$ can be then represented by a convolution kernel:
\begin{equation}
    S(x-x', x') = h(x-x') \quad \mbox{ for all } x, x' \in \mathbb{R}^2
\end{equation}

The image intensity $y$ can be then expressed as a convolution of the amplitude spread function (ASF) $|h|^2$ and the intensity of the sample $|U_o|^2$:
\begin{equation}
    y(x) = (\omega \ast I_o^2)(x) + \boldsymbol{e}(x)
\end{equation}
where $\omega$, is simplified to a function of one variable of $\mathbb{R}^2$.
\subsection{Baseline method: deconvolution}
We propose a baseline estimation of the ASF $\omega$ by using the inverse fourier transform of the ratio of the fourier transform of the measurement $y$ and the fourier transform of the intensity of the sample $I_o^2$:
\begin{equation}
    \hat\omega = \mathcal{F}^{-1}\left(\frac{\mathcal{F}(y)}{\mathcal{F}(I_o^2)}\right)
\end{equation}
This method is simple, yet unstable due to several reasons:
\begin{itemize}
    \item The noise $\boldsymbol{e}$ is amplified by the division of $\mathcal{F}(I_o^2)$, especially at high frequencies where $\mathcal{F}(I_o^2)$ is close to zero.
    \item This estimator implies that the convolution is circular, which is not the case in practice.
\end{itemize}

In the ideal case, where the convolution is circular, we can characterize the bias and variance of the error $\delta \omega$ between the estimator and the true ASF. The bias is given by:
\begin{align}
    \mathbb{E}[\delta\omega] &= \mathbb{E} \left[\mathcal{F}^{-1}\left(\frac{\mathcal{F}(\boldsymbol{e})}{\mathcal{F}(I_o^2)}\right) \right] \\
    & = \mathcal{F}^{-1}\left(\frac{\mathcal{F}(\mathbb{E}[\boldsymbol{e}])}{\mathcal{F}(I_o^2)}\right) \\
    &= 0
\end{align}
The interchange of the inverse fourier transform and the expectation is due to the linearity of the inverse fourier transform.

The expected squared $l_2$ norm of the error $\delta \omega$ is given by:
\begin{align}
    \mathbb{E}[\|\delta\omega\|_{l_2}^2] & = \mathbb{E} \left[\left\|\mathcal{F}^{-1}\left(\frac{\mathcal{F}(\boldsymbol{e})}{\mathcal{F}(I_o^2)}\right) \right\|_{l_2}^2\right] \\
    & = \mathbb{E} \left[\left\|\frac{\mathcal{F}(\boldsymbol{e})}{\mathcal{F}(I_o^2)} \right\|_{l_2}^2\right] 
\end{align}
The second equality is due to the Parseval's theorem, which states that the norm $L_2$ of a function is equal to the norm $l_2$ of its fourier transform. Moreover, one have:
\begin{align}
    \mathbb{E} \left[\left\|\frac{\mathcal{F}(\boldsymbol{e})}{\mathcal{F}(I_o^2)} \right\|_{l_2}^2\right] & = \mathbb{E} \left[\sum_{f=1}^N \left|\frac{\mathcal{F}(\boldsymbol{e})[f]}{\mathcal{F}(I_o^2)[f]}\right|^2\right] \\
    & = \sum_{f=1}^N \frac{1}{|\mathcal{F}(I_o^2)[f]|^2} \, \mathbb{E} \left[|\mathcal{F}(\boldsymbol{e})[f]|^2\right] \\
    & = \sum_{f=1}^N \frac{1}{|\mathcal{F}(I_o^2)[f]|^2} \, \mathbb{E} \left[\mathcal{F}(\boldsymbol{e})[f] \cdot \mathcal{F}(\boldsymbol{e})[-f] \right] \\
    & = \sum_{f=1}^N \frac{1}{|\mathcal{F}(I_o^2)[f]|^2} \, \mathbb{E} \left[\left(\sum_{n=1}^{N}\boldsymbol{e}[n] \exp(-2\pi j f n / N)\right) \cdot \left(\sum_{n=1}^{N}\boldsymbol{e}[n] \exp(2\pi j f n / N)\right)   \right] \\
    & = \sum_{f=1}^N \frac{1}{|\mathcal{F}(I_o^2)[f]|^2} \, \sum_{n=1}^{N} \sum_{m=1}^{N} 
    \mathbb{E}[\boldsymbol{e}[n] \boldsymbol{e}[m]] \exp(-2\pi j f (n-m) / N) \\
    & = \sum_{f=1}^N \frac{1}{|\mathcal{F}(I_o^2)[f]|^2} \, \sum_{n=1}^{N} \sum_{m=1}^{N} \delta_{n,m} \exp(-2\pi j f (n-m) / N) \\
    & = N \sum_{f=1}^N \frac{\sigma^2}{|\mathcal{F}(I_o^2)[f]|^2} \\
\end{align}
As one can observe, the expected squared $l_2$ norm of the error $\delta \omega$ is proportional to the noise variance $\sigma^2$ and inversely proportional to the power of the fourier transform of the intensity of the sample $I_o^2$. This means that the error $\delta \omega$ is amplified at high frequencies where $\mathcal{F}(I_o^2)$ is close to zero, as illustrated in Figure \ref{fig:baseline_estimator}. This error would be even worse if the convolution is not circular, which is usually the case in practice.
